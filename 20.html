HOW THEY TALK

NEW ZEALANDERS speak English with an accent all their own. It is apt to sound a bit like English Cockney to American ears, although it really isn’t. Such words as “now” are given a rather nasal pronunciation like “neow.” Words like “day” sometimes sound a little like “die.” There is a story, probably untrue, of a child who was asked to describe a bison. He said, “A bison is what you wash your hands and fice in!”

You’ll soon learn that the “Z” in New Zealand is pronounced Zed, not Zee. You’ll find out, too, that your New Zealand buddy is no Puritan when it comes to swearing. You hear the word “bloody”—bloody this and bloody that—all over the place, except in mixed company.

As in Australia and Great Britain, many words have different meanings or are used differently. Thus, a biscuit is called a scone and an undershirt is called a singlet. Study the following list carefully, it contains some real surprises.

GLOSSARY OF TERMS

  • Aisle (theatre)—gangway.
  • Apartment house—block of flats.
  • Ash can—dust bin.
  • Ashman—dustman.
  • Atomizer—scent spray.
  • Baggage car—luggage van.
  • Bartender—barman.
  • Bill (money)—banknote or note.
  • Billboard—hoarding.
  • Biscuit—scone or tea cake.
  • Bouncer—chucker out.
  • Broiled (meat)—grilled.
  • Business suit—loungesuit.
  • Candy (hard)—boiled sweets.
  • Candy store—sweet shop.
  • Can opener—tin opener or key.
  • Check baggage—register luggage.
  • Checkers (game)—draughts.
  • Conductor—guard.
  • Closet—cupboard.
  • Coal oil—paraffin.
  • Cookie—biscuit.
  • Corn—maizeor Indian corn.
  • Cotton (absorbent)—cotton wool.
